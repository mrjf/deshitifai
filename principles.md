# Principles of Deshitiffication

Enshittification turns useful systems into extraction machines.
Deshitiffication turns extraction machines back into useful systems.

The opposite of enshittification is not just better UX. It is user-first usefulness: fast, clear, portable, honest, accountable, and boring in the best way.

## The Basic Test

Ask:

- Does this help people get to the thing they came for?
- Does this remove friction, or add it?
- Does this serve the user, or extract from the user?
- Can people understand, copy, save, share, export, question, and leave?
- Does the system get worse when the business model gets hungrier?

## Core Principles

### 1. Make the thing easy to use

- Text should be easy to select and copy.
- Page loads should be fast.
- Links should go directly to the thing.
- Content should not be hidden behind needless interaction.
- Search, sort, filter, print, save, and copy should work like normal.
- The page should work on phones, laptops, old devices, bad browsers, and bad connections.

### 2. Keep advertising and monetization in their place

- Advertising should be minimal.
- Ads should not block, delay, obscure, or reshape the thing people came for.
- Results should be ranked by usefulness, not by who paid.
- Paid placement, affiliate incentives, sponsored content, and commissions should be obvious.
- Do not turn every surface into a store.

### 3. Do not manipulate attention

- No dark patterns, fake urgency, nag modals, hostile popups, or deceptive defaults.
- No addictive loops where a simple answer would do.
- No hidden engagement tuning that makes the thing worse to keep people there longer.
- Avoid autoplay, rage bait, humiliation, outrage amplification, and infinite scroll unless they actually serve the user.

### 4. Preserve human agency

- Make opting out realistic.
- Make settings understandable.
- Make important choices reversible.
- Do not steer, coerce, surveil, profile, or manipulate people into choices they would not otherwise make.
- Do not treat people as profiles, targets, risk scores, engagement units, or data exhaust.

### 5. Make the data portable

- Users should be able to export, save, copy, print, scrape, syndicate, or reuse what they find.
- URLs should be stable, shareable, and bookmarkable.
- Data should be structured where possible.
- Use readable HTML where possible.
- Support feeds, APIs, schemas, archives, and other boring tools that let the web interoperate.
- Do not trap people with lock-in after they have invested time, trust, or data.

### 6. Be transparent about ranking and automation

- Say what is ranked, boosted, hidden, throttled, heated, suppressed, or personalized.
- Algorithms should be explainable enough to distrust.
- AI output should be labeled when it matters.
- AI systems should disclose uncertainty, limitations, and sources where possible.
- High-stakes automated decisions should be reviewable by responsible humans.

### 7. Protect truth

- Important information should have timestamps, sources, and context.
- Corrections should be possible and visible.
- Do not blur advertising, opinion, generated content, and reporting into one mush.
- Do not weaken people's ability to distinguish fact from fiction.
- Design should encourage checking, thinking, and judgment instead of passive dependency.

### 8. Design for the vulnerable first

- Account for disability, age, poverty, low literacy, weak devices, weak connections, and language differences.
- Do not make essential access depend on expensive tools, perfect attention, or technical fluency.
- Use conservative defaults where mistakes or misuse could harm people.
- Treat children, elders, disabled people, migrants, patients, low-income users, and digitally powerless users as first-class users.

### 9. Keep power accountable

- Name who is responsible when things go wrong.
- Provide appeal paths, audit trails, and remedies.
- Do not concentrate essential data, infrastructure, or decision power in a few opaque hands.
- Let affected people shape systems before decisions are locked in.
- Tools encode values, incentives, assumptions, and power. Say what yours are.

### 10. Respect workers and contributors

- Do not hide exploitation in moderation, data labeling, support work, creator labor, delivery work, warehouse work, or supply chains.
- Technology should support workers rather than deskill, surveil, or discard them.
- If automation disrupts work, there should be transition support, worker voice, and realistic adaptation paths.
- Creators, sellers, drivers, writers, maintainers, moderators, and other contributors should not be squeezed after the platform depends on them.

### 11. Use AI as a tedious-work machine, not a control machine

- Use AI to make useful data easier to collect, structure, inspect, and publish.
- Do not use AI to bury decisions, fake certainty, manipulate attention, or replace human responsibility.
- Do not ship a technical answer to a problem that does not need one.
- Be humble about AI. No inevitability narratives. No utopian promises. No "move fast" arrogance.

### 12. Steward the system over time

- Plan for maintenance, repair, migration, portability, and exit.
- Avoid forced dependence on one vendor, one platform, or one fragile integration.
- Count environmental costs: bandwidth, compute, water, energy, devices, waste, and infrastructure.
- Measure whether the thing improves life, not just whether it increases usage.

## Deal-Breaker Flags

A thing is probably not deshitiffied if:

- It makes the user do more work so the platform can extract more value.
- It hides the real answer behind ads, signups, tracking, or interaction sludge.
- It ranks by payment while pretending to rank by quality.
- It blocks copying, saving, exporting, scraping, or sharing for no good reason.
- It uses dark patterns to make leaving, opting out, or cancelling harder.
- It depends on hidden labor exploitation or hidden infrastructure harm.
- It claims neutrality while embedding contested assumptions into ranking, access, pricing, or enforcement.
- It weakens truth, trust, deliberation, or the ability to distinguish fact from fiction.
- It exploits attention, vulnerability, loneliness, addiction, fear, outrage, or confusion.
- It gets worse for users as it gets more profitable.

## Short Version

Deshitiffication means removing everything between the user and the thing they came for.

It means refusing to let the business model slowly eat the usefulness of the thing.
